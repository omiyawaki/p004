1) Key Narrative Claims
- Hot-day amplification relative to mean-day warming is framed as a land-surface problem: intermodel differences in LH/EF response on hot days help explain intermodel spread in warming response.
- Initial working hypothesis: if the Budyko-curve-like EF-SM relation is logistic, time-invariant, and forced by a roughly uniform SM shift, then climatological EF should predict dEF.
- Mid-deck evidence weakens that simple hypothesis: BC shape is not uniformly logistic, BC slope differs across models, and SM PDF change is not a single uniform drying shift.
- Proposed mechanistic framing decomposes dEF into land term (`EF*dP_SM`), atmospheric term (`P_SM*dEF`), and covariance term (`dEF*dP_SM`).

2) Figure-Level Highlights
- Global maps (early STDEV panels) show strongest hot-day-minus-mean warming signals over NH high latitudes and several tropical/subtropical land regions (arrows highlight S/SE Asia and tropical South America).
- Literature context slides (Merrifield et al. 2013; Duan et al. 2020) connect observed precipitation/humidity/latent-heat patterns to hot-extreme behavior over tropical land.
- Intermodel scatter (JJA box near `+13.7,+106.2`) shows strong anti-correlation between hot-day LH/EF change and excess hot-day warming (`R^2` around 0.57 to 0.70 in shown variants).
- Model-by-model EF-SM and PDF/CDF diagnostics show qualitatively different SM distribution responses across models, including near-no-change cases, drying-shift cases, and at least one wetter-shift case (MIROC family).
- Decomposition figures indicate atmospheric term (`P_SM*dEF`) is often positive while land term (`EF*dP_SM`) contributes substantial spread; covariance appears smaller in median.
- KS-statistic-vs-warming scatter (`R^2 ~ 0.42`) suggests larger SM-PDF changes are associated with larger hot-day amplification.

4) Paper-Outline Implications
- Results arc can be structured as: (i) emergent link between hot-day LH/EF response and warming spread, (ii) test/failure of overly simple uniform-shift SM-PDF hypothesis, (iii) decomposition-based attribution of spread to land vs atmospheric contributions.
- Methods section should explicitly define hot-day metric (`T2m^95 - T2m_mean`), EF/LH response diagnostics, SM-PDF change metric (KS), and dEF decomposition terms.
- Discussion should prioritize why models disagree on SM-PDF change (precip/evap partitioning, retention timescale, land parameterization) and whether mean-state EF/SM biases can constrain future hot-extreme response.
