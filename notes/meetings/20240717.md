# 2024-07-17 meeting synthesis

## 1) Key narrative claims
- Core question: why extreme-temperature amplification appears in **tropical summer** but **extratropical winter**.
- Claimed tropical mechanism: amplified **surface turbulent flux repartitioning** (especially SH/LH structure) during warm season.
- Follow-up mechanism question: why hot-day evaporative cooling (LH response at high percentiles) is amplified in tropics.
- Process-diagnosis approach: use BC-based decomposition (`LH`, `LH_BC`, `LH_BC_hist`) to separate pathway effects.
- Open methodological pivot: possibly reframe from "warming relative to monthly median" to "warming at each percentile relative to annual-mean warming."

## 2) Figure-level interpretation highlights
- **Fig. 1 (ANN + seasonal percentile structure):**
  - Tropics: upper-percentile warming anomaly tends positive, lower-percentile anomaly tends negative/weakly negative.
  - Extratropics: opposite-signed tails with stronger seasonality; winter-month structure is more pronounced.
- **Fig. 2 (tropical energy-budget decomposition):**
  - `Δ∂SH` and `Δ∂LH` are emphasized (red boxes) as dominant contributors vs weaker `Δ∂G`.
  - Seasonal phasing suggests repartitioning between sensible and latent flux contributes to percentile-dependent warming.
- **Fig. 3 (BC/pathway diagnostics):**
  - `LH_BC_hist` sits offset from `LH_BC`/`Actual LH` in parts of the seasonal cycle, prompting explicit "what causes this offset?" slide.
  - Deck treats this as unresolved: either explain physically (e.g., wind changes) or deprioritize if seasonally flat.
- **Late-deck framing slides (monthly-median vs annual-mean baseline):**
  - Annual-mean framing is argued to better expose physical mechanisms and societal relevance (e.g., tropical hot-season warming), while monthly-median framing is standard in extremes literature.
