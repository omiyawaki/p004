1) Key Narrative Claims
- The core claim is a seasonal asymmetry in high-latitude percentile warming: winter warm-tail suppression versus summer warm-tail amplification.
- In winter high latitudes, suppressed warming near the hottest percentiles is tied to temperatures near freezing, where added energy is diverted into phase change rather than sensible warming.
- In summer high latitudes, hot-percentile amplification persists, but with a different budget structure than winter (less direct melt control at the warm tail).
- CESM2 broadly reproduces the multi-model mean behavior for the headline winter/summer contrasts, then extends analysis to shoulder seasons and other latitude bands.
- The deck implicitly argues for a regime-based explanation (freezing-point constrained vs non-constrained parts of the distribution), not a single mechanism across all seasons/regions.

2) Figure-Level Highlights
- Slide 1: High-latitude AI-percentile x month heatmap shows sign reversal by season (blue in winter months, positive anomalies in warm season months), motivating mechanism analysis.
- Slides 2-6: PDFs and percentile-warming curves show winter warm-tail suppression near ~273 K and summer warm-tail amplification; CESM2 retains this contrast.
- Slides 7-10: Winter CESM2 energy-budget diagnostics show strong negative ΔFmelt concentrated at top percentiles, co-located with the sharp drop in ΔT at the warm tail.
- Slide 11: High-latitude summer has a low-percentile dip in ΔT (~10th-20th percentile) with a pronounced ΔFmelt spike structure there; warm-tail amplification is mainly outside that melt-sensitive band.

- Slides 14-15 and 21-22: Midlatitudes show weaker but still structured non-monotonic behavior and budget compensation across ΔRn, ΔSH, ΔLH, with episodic ΔFmelt influence.
- Slides 19-20 and 23: Tropics show near-zero melt contribution across most percentiles; ΔT patterns track radiative/turbulent partitioning more than phase-change energetics.
- Slide 24: Historical/future percentile-temperature curves (high-lat winter/summer) visually confirm distribution shifts and a summer kink near lower-mid percentiles consistent with earlier diagnostics.

4) Paper-Outline Implications
- Mechanism section: center the winter high-latitude story on freezing-point energy partitioning (ΔFmelt sink) suppressing warm-tail warming.
- Contrast section: explicitly separate summer high-latitude amplification mechanisms from winter suppression, rather than forcing a unified budget narrative.
- Comparative section: add latitude-band contrasts (high latitudes, midlatitudes, tropics) to show where phase-change control is dominant vs negligible.
- Methods/robustness section: document treatment of percentile-edge noise and justify any percentile-window restriction (consistent with the slide-9 note).
- Discussion section: frame shoulder-season non-monotonicity as a transition-regime test of the freezing-threshold mechanism, not just an extension of winter/summer endpoints.
