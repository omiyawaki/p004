1) Key Narrative Claims
- Under ~2 K global warming (end-century framing), hot-day amplification is spatially structured, with strongest annual-mean enhancement over central South America (slide 4).
- Seasonal maxima of hot-day amplification are emphasized as a stronger diagnostic than annual mean, highlighting tropical/subtropical hotspots (SE Asia, South America, southern Africa; slides 5-7).
- Mechanistic claim: regions with energy-limited mean conditions but water-limited hot-day conditions preferentially produce amplified hot-day warming (concept + mask on slides 9-11).
- The South America January LH-SM analysis argues robustness of a piecewise LH-SM curve to resampling (`n=50..1000`) and to inclusion/exclusion of added regime-shape classes (`0101`, `1101`; slides 12-21), though impact appears modest in shown examples.
- Intermodel-spread attribution: covariance structure links hot-day warming spread more with LH/SH/radiative flux spread patterns; later synthesis claims spread in LH-SM relationship matters more than spread in SM itself (slides 27-30, 50-57).

2) Figure-Level Highlights
- Slide 4: global map of enhanced hot-day warming (`~ -0.9 to 0.9 K`) with clear positive center over South America and negative high-latitude patches.
- Slide 5: seasonal-maximum map broadens/strengthens hotspot signal; red-circled regions align with subsequent regional narrative.
- Slides 9-11: paired line/map visuals clearly show seasonal hot-day peak and a regime-transition interpretation (mean vs hot-day `SM-SM_crit` behavior), then a global boolean mask for mean energy-limited + hot-day water-limited conditions.
- Slides 12-15 and 18-21: Central South America January LH-SM panels show stable MMM piecewise shape across bootstrap size and little first-order change when removing candidate added classes.
- Slides 27, 29-30: intermodel spread/correlation maps show mostly negative `R(Δ∂T,Δ∂LH)` and positive `R(Δ∂T,Δ∂SH)` over many land regions, with mixed radiative terms.
- Slides 33-49: mean/hot/extreme (`T̄`, `T95`, `δT95`) energy-flux map sets consistently show land-contrast structure; hot-minus-mean flux anomalies are strongest in tropical/subtropical land bands.
- Slide 51: side-by-side "actual hot-mean LH response" vs BC-derived estimate reports spatial correlation `R = 0.74`.
- Slide 57: final map pair supports stated conclusion that LH-SM-relationship spread explains LH spread better than SM spread alone.

3) Reliability/Artifact Flags
- Presenter notes are sparse and uneven: only slides 4-5 contain substantive prose; slides 6/7/9 show truncated fragment "In southeast" without completion, suggesting unfinished notes/template carryover rather than reliable narrative evidence.
- Most `meeting_notes` pages contain only slide thumbnails (no independent narration), so extracted notes text is largely duplicated slide text.
- Several analysis sections rely on image-only pages (e.g., 33, 35, 37, 39, 41, 43, 45, 47-49, 53, 55); text extraction alone would materially miss evidence.
- The class-inclusion sensitivity test (0101/1101 kept vs removed) appears visually small in presented examples; claim of major sensitivity is not strongly demonstrated here.
- Metadata dates are 2026 export timestamps, not original meeting-day provenance.

4) Paper-Outline Implications
- Structure results around a two-step story: (i) where/when hot-day amplification is largest, then (ii) why via LH-SM regime shifts between mean and hot days.
- Keep South America January as a worked mechanistic case, but quantify sensitivity effects (not just visual overlays) if this is to carry methodological weight.
- Use the flux-correlation and decomposition blocks as attribution support, with explicit caveats that relationships are correlational and spatially heterogeneous.
- Promote the BC-vs-actual `R=0.74` result as a mid-strength validation, not full closure; pair it with uncertainty language before broad mechanistic claims.
- In manuscript drafting, treat presenter-note prose as low-confidence ancillary context; prioritize claims directly supported by plotted diagnostics.
