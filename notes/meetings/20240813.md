# 20240813 meeting review

## 1) Key narrative claims
- Main scientific question: why warming of extremes is amplified in tropical summer and extratropical winter.
- Working claim is that tropical hot-day amplification is consistent with stronger surface turbulent flux repartitioning, especially latent heat (LH).
- A modeling/process issue is emphasized: BC-based LH decomposition has an offset between `Histonly BC LH`, `Hist+fut BC LH`, and `Actual LH`; this complicates physical interpretation.
- Three framing options are explicitly debated:
  - explain the BC offset physically (example given: wind-speed change),
  - ignore offset if it lacks seasonal structure,
  - reframe around warming at each percentile (vs relative-to-median framing).
- Later slides compare two anomaly definitions: warming anomaly from monthly median warming (extremes-focused convention) vs annual-mean warming (argued to better expose seasonal-mean mechanisms and societal relevance).

## 4) Implications for paper outline
- Recommended core arc:
  - Problem statement: seasonal asymmetry of extreme warming (tropics summer vs extratropics winter).
  - Mechanistic diagnosis: turbulent flux repartitioning (SH/LH) with BC decomposition as a process test.
  - Methodological framing: explicit comparison of anomaly baselines (monthly-median vs annual-mean) and what each reveals.
  - Decision point: either resolve BC offset mechanism or formally bound its impact and proceed with percentile-centric framing.
- Practical writing implication:
  - Keep one main framing choice in the paper to avoid mixed messaging; current deck signals active indecision.
  - If annual-mean baseline is adopted, motivate with seasonal-mean physics and societal relevance; if monthly-median baseline is retained, emphasize continuity with extremes literature.
