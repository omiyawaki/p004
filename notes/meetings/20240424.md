1) Key Narrative Claims
- The deck argues that percentile-by-season structure in near-surface temperature (`T`) is closely linked to lower-tropospheric horizontal temperature advection variability, and that this link strengthens when using binned (nonlinear) slope estimates instead of a single linear slope.
- A central claim is methodological: month-specific nonlinear behavior (shown with January and May examples) implies that piecewise/binned sensitivity is more physically informative than global linear regression.
- The transition from “historical slope” to “historical + future binned slope” suggests that adding future climate states changes inferred sensitivities, especially at percentile extremes and in shoulder seasons.
- The `aG` and `a_hG` pathways are presented as alternative transformed predictors that can reproduce broad `T` season-percentile structure, with different spatial fingerprints than advection-only forms.
- High-latitude seasonal-contrast maps (`DJF+JJA`, and an extra `JJA+DJF` view) are used to claim that thermodynamic warming (`ΔδT`) is smoother while transformed-dynamic terms (`ΔδaG`, `Δδa_hG`, etc.) are spatially patchier and regionally sign-changing.

3) Reliability/Artifact Flags
- The `meeting_notes` PDF appears to be slide snapshots without additional speaker-note prose, so interpretation relies on figure inference rather than explicit author commentary.
- Heavy hatching across many mid-percentile cells suggests limited robustness there; strongest claims should focus on unhatched edge-percentile/season features.
- Binned/piecewise fits improve visual alignment in case studies, but bin count/placement is not documented on-slide; sensitivity to bin design is a key uncertainty.
- Residual-metric changes (skew/kurtosis) are shown for January only; generalizing improvement to all months is not yet demonstrated.
- Several transformed fields (`aG`, `a_hG`, `a_h`-advection products) have patchy sign-alternating maps that could reflect sampling noise, masking choices, or derivative amplification rather than robust dynamics.
- Visual narrative depends on repeated colorbar-limited heatmaps; conclusions may shift with alternative scaling/normalization.
- Sign conventions are nontrivial (`-(u c_p ∂xT + v c_p ∂yT)` forms); any inconsistency across scripts/figures could invert physical interpretation.

4) Paper-Outline Implications
- Lead with a methods-forward framing: motivate why percentile-season structure requires nonlinear (binned) sensitivity, then compare against linear baseline as a controlled benchmark.
- Build results in tiers: (i) baseline `T` and advection heatmaps, (ii) month case studies (January, May) demonstrating nonlinearity, (iii) all-month sensitivity seasonality, (iv) transformed predictors (`aG`, `a_hG`) and spatial pattern consequences.
- Add a robustness subsection explicitly testing binning choices, significance masks, and historical-only vs historical+future fit stability.
- Separate high-confidence statements (seasonal slope contrasts, edge-percentile organization, nonlinear `G` relation) from provisional ones (fine-scale mapped sign reversals in transformed terms).
- Include a diagnostics appendix with residual statistics for all months (not just January) and with clear sign-convention checks traceable to code.
- For synthesis figures, pair each transformed-term map with uncertainty/significance overlays so patchy anomalies are not over-interpreted.
